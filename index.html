About Me

I am an undergraduate at the University of Arizona in applied math with a minor in optics. I'm currently researching in the Quantum Optomechanics Lab and the Quantum Photonics and Nanophotonics Group for my honors thesis, focused on guided mode resonance devices. Additionally, I am an R&D intern at Fringe Metrology, working on rapid-scanning metrology systems for radio telescopes.

Projects

  • FMCW LiDAR Metrology System

    As an R&D intern, I led a testing and design project of a new metrology tool using an accurate FMCW LiDAR for rapid-scanning methods on large radio dishes for the Next Generation Very Large Array (ngVLA) radio telescopes. This project, if proven successful, could advance to a SBIR Phase II for further development. I worked on pushing the sensor's limits and designing a complete system with dynamic focus and beam steering.

    Skills: Optical Systems, Zemax, Scripting, Data Analysis

    Supervised by Dr. Joel Berkson

  • Simulation and Modeling of Guided Mode Resonance Device

    This academic year, I am undertaking an honors thesis project focusing on enhancing the precision and sensitivity of rotation sensors through innovative use of silicon nitride-based GMR devices. The project involves rigorous design, simulation, and modeling of these devices to detect rotational movements with high accuracy, which is crucial for applications in nanomechanical systems. Expected to culminate next spring, this project aims to push the boundaries of current capabilities in rotation sensing.

    Advised by Dr. Dalziel Wilson & Dr. Mohamed ElKabbash

  • Radio Telescope Manufacturing

    As part of a collaborative team, I contributed to constructing a radio telescope, focusing on precision engineering to support astronomical research. My responsibilities included adjusting molds to sub-40 micron accuracy, analyzing thermoforming processes with MATLAB for optimized shaping of aluminum panels, and advancing an automation project integrating a PID-controlled DC motor for precise mold adjustments. This work demonstrated significant potential for advancements in radio astronomy infrastructure.

    Skills: CAD, MATLAB, Control Systems, Arduino

    SPIE Presentation

    Advised by Dr. Justin Hyatt & Dr. Daewook Kim

  • Cost-Effective Method for Polarization Characterization

    In this project, I explored innovative, budget-friendly methods in polarimetry, an optics branch crucial for analyzing light's polarization properties. Utilizing a setup with a CMOS sensor-equipped polarimetry camera and various optical elements, I successfully characterized linear polarization states of light, integrating data processing techniques in NumPy and Matplotlib to calculate and visualize Stokes parameters. This study advances polarimetry by offering a low-cost, power-efficient alternative suitable for small-scale space missions.

    Skills: Raspberry Pi, Camera Communication, Data Analysis, Experimental Design
