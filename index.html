FMCW LiDAR Metrology System

As an R&D intern, I led a testing and designing project of a new metrology tools using an accurate FMCW LiDAR for rapid-scanning methods on big radio dishes for the Next Generation Very Large Array (ngVLA) radio telescopes. A positive proof-of-concept of this project can push this project into a SBIR Phase II for further development. As part of this work I've pushed the limits of the sensor and designed a complete system that includes a dynamical focus and beam steering.

  • Skills: Optical Systems, Zemax, Scripting, Data Analysis

Supervised by Dr. Joel Berkson

Simulation and Modeling of Guided Mode Resonance Device

This academic year, I am undertaking an honors thesis project focusing on enhancing the precision and sensitivity of rotation sensors through the innovative use of silicon nitride-based GMR devices. This work involves the rigorous design, simulation, and modeling of these devices to detect rotational movements with high accuracy, crucial for applications in nanomechanical systems. Expected to culminate next spring, this project not only fulfills my thesis requirement but also aims to set new standards in optical sensor technology, pushing the boundaries of current capabilities in rotation sensing.

Advised by Dr. Dalziel Wilson & Dr. Mohamed ElKabbash

Radio Telescope Manufacturing

As part of a collaborative team, I contributed to the construction of a radio telescope, focusing on precision engineering to support astronomical research. My responsibilities included adjusting molds to sub-40 micron accuracy, analyzing thermoforming processes using MATLAB to optimize the shaping of aluminum panels, and advancing an automation project that integrated a PID-controlled DC motor for precise mold adjustments. This work not only advanced the assembly and functionality of the telescope but also demonstrated potential for future enhancements in radio astronomy infrastructure. Although I've stopped working in this project this past August, I am excited about its fulfillment this fall!

  • Skills: CAD, MATLAB, Control Systems, Arduino, Scripting

Advised by Dr. Justin Hyatt & Dr. Daewook Kim

Cost-Effective Method for Polarization Characterization

In this project, I explored innovative, budget-friendly methods in polarimetry—an optics branch crucial for analyzing light's polarization properties. Utilizing a novel setup involving a CMOS sensor-equipped polarimetry camera and various optical elements, I successfully characterized the linear polarization states of light, integrating data processing techniques in NumPy and Matplotlib to calculate and visualize Stokes parameters. This study significantly advances polarimetry by offering a less costly, power-efficient alternative suitable for small-scale space missions and beyond. The promising results underscore the potential of these techniques to make sophisticated polarization analysis more accessible, setting the stage for future work to extend these methods to characterize circular and elliptical polarization states.

  • Skills: Raspberry Pi, Camera Communication, Data Analysis, Experimental Design

Advised by Dr. Jess Johnson & Dr. Ewan Douglas
